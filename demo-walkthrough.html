Box · Sr PM, AI Agent Orchestration — Round 1
30-minute walkthrough · Product / Engineering / Design panel

From describing conversations to closing the loop.

A run-of-show for the ConnectWork ConnectAgent — a governed work-orchestration layer. This page is my presenter cockpit and your leave-behind: every beat, the exact line, the live UX, and where each moment is real (not scripted).

The pitch in one breath: Today's Conversational Insights Agent describes conversations. I'm turning it into the trusted layer that closes the loop from discussed to done — it drafts the decision, the substrate governs every output, then it fans the work across the team and tracks whether the result is still true. The hard part isn't a smarter model; it's an orchestration substrate that makes every output permission-safe, deterministically verified, and audit-ready. That substrate is the platform this role is hired to build.
Job 1 — Growth

Close the work loop

Move past summarization into governed work products + safe actions, then orchestrate them across owners. The loop (discussed→done) is the productivity unlock customers actually pay for.

Job 2 — Trust

Stop the churn

Regulated customers leave because AI can't be trusted for approvals, calculations, and audits. A deterministic substrate is what wins them back. RAG finds content; it doesn't prove the reasoning is right.

★ The "rock star" bar

Most candidates will show three feature mockups and a roadmap. I'll show a running governed system and let the panel type into it live — block a discount, route it, watch it revalidate, then fan the follow-ups across four owners — and prove the platform generalizes across three regulated verticals on one substrate. The artifact is the differentiation.

Flipping the "customer-sense" read

The AI depth is a given. So I lead with the user: Dana, a Relationship Manager, 30 minutes before a credit committee, trying to do her job. Every beat ties to her job-to-be-done, the reluctant user, and the buyer's audit risk — not model internals.

Assumptions (stated, per the brief): synthetic Acme scenario + personas; real orchestration / gates / evals. Numbers are internally consistent across memo, brief, gates, actions, the batch, and the sealed record.

DECISION

Yes — open with a 75-second Screen Studio cold open

Recommendation with rationale, because a panel will silently judge whether you can run a clean demo.

Why record the cold open
  • De-risks the live demo. If Zoom screen-share, the gateway, or the network hiccups, the panel has already seen it work end-to-end. The live portion becomes upside, not a single point of failure.
  • Sets the arc. 75 seconds of the full loop (refuse → brief → revalidate → act → batch → seal → platform) gives the panel a map, so the deep live portion reads as "showing the seams," not "finding my way."
  • Buys interactivity. With the "what" pre-shown, I spend live minutes on the convincing part: handing them the keyboard.
Keep it ≤ 90s. The brief wants a live walkthrough; the recording is the amuse-bouche, not the meal.

The deep-dive — what they'll push on, and my answers

I'll invite them to role-play departments (Eng/Design/GTM) and dig. Pre-loaded answers, each with an "I built this" hook.

?Isn't this just RAG with extra steps?
RAG is necessary, not sufficient. It finds content; it doesn't validate an approval matrix, recompute a covenant ratio, or prove an action is safe. Published financial-reasoning benchmarks put RAG-with-reranking near ~50% factual correctness; a neurosymbolic layer reaches ~95%. And the symbolic layer doesn't just sit on top of retrieval — it improves it: RuleRAG reports an ~89% Recall@10 improvement from using symbolic rules to guide retrieval (RuleRAG's figure, cited in Saxena et al. 2026, arXiv 2601.04568 — emerging research, not consensus), and the Saxena survey argues this matters most in high-stakes domains where opaque retrieval erodes trust. So the symbolic layer improves retrieval AND owns the pass/fail — you watched the arithmetic, not a vibe.
Hook: understander → planner → verifier → executor with explicit typed contracts.
?The "Agent Batch" — is that real orchestration or a scripted animation?
Real. The batch is a typed loop — assignments to typed owners, replies with a decision enum, escalations, scheduled items, and an audit — the same shape the gateway returns from POST /actions/loop. The Legal→Compliance escalation is derived from authority being exceeded, and the "1 item open" banner reads escalations-in-flight, not a hardcoded "closed" flag.
Hook: closed means the cycle ran; open status is computed from escalations, so a closed batch can still be honestly unfinished.
?Aren't you competing with Microsoft Copilot?
On regulated work-product trust, yes — permissions, source graph, deterministic gates, action diffs, multi-owner orchestration, audit, revalidation. Not on generic copilot parity. The moat is Box's native content + workflow + permission graph; a horizontal assistant can't deterministically gate what it doesn't own.
Hook: permission filter runs before content enters the prompt — denied content never reaches the model.
?Is this cross-app agent orchestration?
Not as the first wedge. We make Box's own work graph actionable and trustworthy, and reach external systems only through typed connectors — we're the governed layer other agents call into via MCP. That's also the headless direction the platform is heading.
Hook: ToolCard registry with side-effect classes + approval policy per tool.
?How do you evaluate without inspecting customer prompts and content?
Layered and content-free: synthetic evals, tenant-local runners (raw stays in the tenant), consented redacted samples, AI Studio feedback, and differential-privacy aggregates. The honest caveat: DP helps aggregate trend learning, not row-level debugging — that's what tenant-local + redacted samples are for. You saw the trace: typed signals only, enforced by schema.
Hook: offline-replay eval harness; accept/edit/reject telemetry becomes the regression set.
?Why finance first — isn't it over-engineered?
Finance has the clearest churn signal and the richest deterministic surface — calculations, approvals, MNPI, auditability. It's also where the regulation is most concrete: credit scoring is a named Annex III high-risk category under the EU AI Act, and Article 14 requires a human-interpretable record of the factors behind each decision — so the "over-engineering" is the buyer's compliance requirement, not gold-plating. The depth is intentional, but the primitives are platform primitives, which is why the demo ends with Legal + Health on the same substrate. Configuration, not code.
Hook: verticals differ only by RulePack + recipe + eval pack.
?Single most important deterministic component?
The approval / policy gate. It directly addresses the churn-driving trust gap — and it's the one place you must never let the model decide. You saw it hold when Dana tried to apply the discount herself, when she approved every action at once, and after a sign-off when two blockers remained.
Hook: the gate is enforced in the wrapper and re-run server-side on execute; any model "approved" text is neutralized.
?What would you cut if scope got tight?
Cut full AI Studio to a static Phase-3 mock. Keep Phase-0 substrate, the Decision Brief, the deterministic policy block, live revalidation, the Agent Actions drawer, the Agent Batch loop, the sealed governed record + stale alert, eval traces, and the three-vertical proof. The trust spine is non-negotiable; the authoring UI can wait.
Hook: I sequence by what earns trust, not what demos flashiest.
?Design: what about the user who didn't opt into AI?
The agent proposes; the human owns the go. Provenance on every claim, a visible "what I couldn't use," no forced default-accept, reversible diffs, and an honest "still not ready" when it isn't. The reluctant user gets an assistant that's auditable, not an autopilot they distrust. Adoption is a change-management problem as much as a model problem.
Hook: every action carries a before/after diff + required approver + rollback.
